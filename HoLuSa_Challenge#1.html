Context of use

In the current case, the context of use of the suggested application is to make it easier for students in terms of the accessibility of navigation in the city, as well as offering solutions for people who have disabilities and do not have the necessary conditions to carry out their activity.

During the exploration, we discovered the fact that there are certain problems that can affect the efficiency of some simple problems in a student's life, but that can be solved by implementing simple solutions that can make a difference. Most of the problems are related to the nature of the transport, the conditions offered being sometimes insufficient, but what we noticed more strongly is the fact that students with disabilities are deprived of certain things that help them in carrying out their daily activities, such as disabled ramps, elevators and the presence of many scales within the faculty.

Main audience

Given the above problems, we focused on creating an application that can come up with a solution for some problems, our main audience being students, students who use their personal car to get to faculty and students with disabilities.

Empiric laws

Fitts' law is an effective method of modeling user performance in rapid, aimed movements, where one appendage (like a hand) starts at rest at a specific start position, and moves to rest within a target area.

Its principles are:

  • Things done more often should be assigned a larger button
  • Things done more often should be closer to the average position of the user's cursor
  • The top, bottom, and sides of the screen are infinitely targetable because of the boundary created by the edges of the screen

The application must be as intuitive and easy to use as possible. A very good example would be the use of a main menu with redirection to the student who prefers public transport / the student who uses his personal car and the student with disabilities. Each of this category will have presented the options provided by the application

In the section "Student who prefers public transport", we will have representative buttons for the map of public transport and the option to reserve a place where a short questionnaire will be used with: the departure station and the selection of the preferred departure time

In the section "Student who uses his personal car,we will have representative buttons for the map that will select the shortest route and the possibility of reserving a parking space in the parking lot specially arranged for students by entering the student identification number. If there are no parking spaces available, the student will be notified with "No parking space available" and will automatically be presented with the option to search for a parking space near the faculty."

In the section "Student with disabilities,we will have representative buttons for asking a colleague for help. There will be 2 fields to enter the current location and the destination and also the option to reserve a place identical to the questionnaire in the section Student who prefers public transport".

Registration in the application to help colleagues in need will be done on the basis of a questionnaire with name/surname/email address or phone number/faculty/student identification number. Each registered student will receive a notification either by SMS or email and the one who accepts the request for help will have a simple button that will notify the student with disabilities that his request has been accepted and in the shortest time the Name + Surname colleague will come to help.

Once the selected profile is selected, it will remain selected, but it can be changed at any time from the main menu. Also, the route used most often will be saved to favorites after a few uses of it, valid both for the route of the student who prefers public transport and for the student who prefers the car.

Hick’s Law states that the more choices a person is presented with, the longer the person will take to reach a decision.

There are exceptions to Hick’s Law. For one, it applies only to equally probable choices, where the user is equally likely to select any of the choices. This means that if users already know what they want to do before seeing the list of choices, the time it takes them to act is likely to be less than what Hick’s Law describes.

From the moment the student selects his profile, the list of available options is not very large, and the fact that he already knows for what purpose he is using the application, whether he wants to reserve a seat in a means of transport, or whether he wants to find a faster route to college/a parking space or need help, the reaction time is instant.

The Steering law suggests that the size and location of graphical targets in an interface can significantly affect the ease and efficiency with which users can interact with them.

In practice, this means that designers should strive to create interfaces with larger and more accessible targets, such as buttons, sliders, and other interactive elements. By making these elements easier to select and interact with, designers can reduce the time required for users to complete tasks and improve overall user experience.

All three of these laws are important in the design of interfaces and interactive systems, as they can inform decisions about the size and placement of targets, the number of options presented to users, and the overall complexity of the interface. By understanding these principles, designers can create interfaces that are more efficient, intuitive, and easy to use, thing we try as much as possible through the simplicity of the menu and the options we provide.

Usability problems of various physical and digital things of interest

Primary type:

  1. Lack of parking spaces or specially arranged parking lots near the faculty
  2. Inability to use the only elevator available in the faculty due to crowding
  3. The lack of several access roads to the classrooms, as the main stairs are often crowded and there is no streamlining of their use
  4. The lack of a mean of public transport specially designed for students, useful especially during rush hours, to avoid traffic

Secondary type:

  1. The lack of access ramps for students with disabilities
  2. The lack of a special elevator for disabled people, especially due to the fact that there are only stairs leading to the classrooms

How could metaphors/idioms, affordances, and visual variables be used?

Metaphors and idioms

Idioms and metaphors are effective literary tools used to communicate everyday.The elevator for example of an effective mataphor. Learning how to use it is effortless and memorable. There are no tutorials for using it. We don’t understand the technical functionality of the elevator perfectly, but we use it almost everyday. This is metaphoric and idiomatic learning.

The icons used in the application will be intuitive, representing a means of public transport / a car / a person with disabilities, precisely to make the selection obvious.

Affordances

Affordances resonate with designers concern about making possible uses of their products immediately obvious. For instance, we can easily see the door handle or the stairs we have to climb to get to faculty, which we use correctly without thinking. Another intuitive example could be the elevator menu which can include strong clues, such as clickable buttons and tabs, draggable sliders, and spinnable controls, as well as other elements that more or less directly suggest suitable user actions.

An example in our application is the menu consisting of three main profiles which then redirects the user to his area of interest.

The use of empirical laws of interaction, affordances, and flow

Flow experience is the main variable for maintaining a long-lasting, sustainable relationship with the users.

The most determining factors of the users’ flow state are:

  • the ease of use
  • the type of content

Flow refers to the state of being fully immersed in an activity or experience. In the context of user experience design, flow refers to designing interfaces and experiences that allow users to become fully absorbed in what they are doing. This can be achieved by designing interfaces that are intuitive, responsive, and engaging. By designing interfaces that facilitate flow, designers can help users to become more productive, engaged, and satisfied with the experience.

Together, empirical laws of interaction, affordances and flow can inform the design of interfaces that are both efficient and enjoyable to use. By understanding the empirical laws of interaction, designers can create interfaces that are intuitive and easy to use. By incorporating affordances into the design, designers can make it easier for users to understand how to interact with an interface. Finally, by designing interfaces that induce flow, designers can create experiences that are engaging and satisfying for users.
